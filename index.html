Welcome to the Nikkei Internment Memorial Centre

Nikkei (nee-kay): A Japanese term for people of Japanese heritage whose ancestors left Japan to settle in another country and start a new life. The term distinguishes those who remained in Japan (Nihonjin) from those born or established elsewhere (Nikkei).

At the time of internment, Japanese Canadians had lived along the West Coast of British Columbia for sixty-five years, raising two generations of Canadian-born children. The majority of the more than 22,000 Nikkei interned in Canada during WWII were Canadian citizens by birth.

At the end of the war, all internees were exiled from British Columbia “east of the Rocky Mountains” or shipped to Japan. They could not return until 1949. Japanese Canadians refer to this as the “Second Uprooting.”

All internment camps were closed and bulldozed, except for this New Denver “Orchard” camp. Approximately 2,000 infirm Japanese Canadians were transferred to the Orchard, occupying shacks vacated by healthy internees who were forcibly removed.

Japanese Canadians received a Federal Redress Settlement in 1988, and in 2022, British Columbia formally recognized the injustices committed by the Province.

This centre is a place of remembrance. Here, we honour the Japanese Canadians who suffered, bear witness to their struggle, and commit to learning from this history so it does not repeat itself.

Visitor Reception Centre

The original internment shacks were timbered, milled, and constructed by Japanese Canadian internees to a uniform size of 14’ x 28’. A single entry with a gabled dormer identifies these structures, designed as basic shelter. Although the fir shiplap siding has weathered over time, the shrinkage caused by the use of “green” lumber remains clearly visible.

The interior of this structure has been fully renovated to serve as the Visitors’ Reception Centre.

This community billboard originally stood in front of the Kyowakai Hall and was relocated in 1994. British Columbia Security Commission notices for the “Orchard” were posted here. Later, the billboard served to inform internees about cultural and social events.

Fire and Water Display

These fire bucket stands were placed throughout the Orchard. They were the only firefighting equipment available during internment. The buckets had rounded bottoms to discourage use for other purposes. They were filled with sand, and water was used when available.

At first, water was hauled from the lake by hand. Standpipes were installed in 1943. A single pipe supplied four to six households with water for drinking, cooking, washing, and laundry. The “L”-shaped bar extending from the wooden box connected to a valve below the frost line, controlling the flow of water to the spigot.

Carved Stump

Japanese Canadian Fire Fighters

The names carved into this cedar tree belong to Japanese Canadian Firefighters from the Lemon Creek Internment Camp in the Slocan Valley. On July 28, 1945, they were sent to fight a forest fire on the west side of the Arrow Lakes, across from the Village of Nakusp.

This tree was discovered by Pope & Talbot Logging. The stump, along with its history, was donated to the Nikkei Internment Memorial Centre.

Shack 1942–1944

The exterior of this original building shows later modifications, including cedar shakes and sliding windows. These changes were likely made after the first winter. The earliest shacks were erected quickly to shelter women and children from the snow, as many were initially placed in tents until the shacks were completed.

The interior has been divided into two interpretive areas: the space to the left represents conditions when internees were first housed in 1942; the space to the right represents living conditions in 1943. This dwelling typically housed two families, often with up to six children each. In many cases, the families did not know each other before internment.

Outhouse

In 1942, when women, children, and the elderly were moved to New Denver, there were no outhouses on the barren Orchard delta. Facilities were not built until 1943, after the men completed the shacks and rejoined their families.

One outhouse served four to six households, or up to fifty people. Outhouses were divided into a men’s side and a women’s side. These were the only toilets available in the Orchard.

Septic systems and indoor plumbing were installed later in the nineteen fifties.

Shack 1944–1957

This structure depicts conditions in 1957, when one family lived in each shack.

In 1957, the Kyowakai Society negotiated a resolution with the British Columbia Security Commission. The Commission kept the confiscated funds, and in exchange, the Kyowakai Society requested that the “shack and pedestal of land” be deeded to surviving internees occupying shacks in the Orchard.

The Kyowakai Society then worked with the Village of New Denver to move the shacks along the lakefront and the TB Sanatorium into the neighborhood of lots and streets as they exist today. Acting on behalf of Japanese Canadian property owners, the Society deeded the vacated lakefront land to the Village of New Denver to be used, in perpetuity, as public lands.

The deeds of approximately 280 parcels were recorded in the Province’s Lands and Title Offices and remain a matter of public record.

Vegetable Garden

The Orchard Crown land, leased by the British Columbia Security Commission as an internment site, was first leased in the 1800s to grow apples. By 1900, orchardists had moved to the Okanagan, where fruit export was easier.

In 1942, the Orchard was an uncultivated, barren field with no fruit trees. In the spring of 1943, the Doukhobor community helped internees learn planting methods suited to the interior climate to produce fresh food.

This garden includes fuki, a plant carried by Japanese Canadian internees into the interior. Its stalk is used in many Japanese dishes. Japanese Canadians also gathered wild mushrooms (“matsutake”) and fiddleheads from the surrounding forest.

After the 1957 settlement, Japanese Canadians planted vegetable and flower gardens on their properties.

Peace Arch

This is a replica of the original Peace Arch, built in 1945 by internees transferred to New Denver. It stood at the entrance to the “San”, the only medical facility available to Japanese Canadians during and after WWII.

The structure reflects a Coast Salish design, developed through Coast Salish and Japanese Canadian collaboration around their shared harbours, shipyards, and canneries along the Pacific coast. The gateway and fence symbolize peace and harmony: the arch forms a “bridge of welcome”, and the fence signifies a link between people of different nations.

Internees built the Peace Arch to affirm their identity as coastal people, and to mark that many remained in the Orchard after the war because they were too frail to be exiled from British Columbia.

In 1953, the government demolished and burned the Peace Arch when it leased the vacated sanatorium to operate as a residential school for Doukhobor children.

Centennial Hall

In 1977, Kyowakai Society president Mrs Chie Kamegaya registered the Society under the British Columbia Societies Act. As an official society, she applied to the Broader Horizons program to build a new meeting hall, since the Kyowakai Hall had fallen into disrepair and could not serve large gatherings. At the time, more than one hundred Japanese Canadians still lived in shacks in the Orchard.

Construction was planned for 1977. The hall was originally intended to be named Centennial Hall as part of national Japanese Canadian Centennial celebrations marking one hundred years of Japanese Canadian residency in Canada. The first Issei (first emigrant Nikkei) to settle in Canada was Manzo Nagano, who arrived on the British Columbia coast in 1877. The centennial celebration helped build momentum for a national redress settlement.

During planning, Mrs Kamegaya was advised that the name “Centennial Hall” might jeopardize approval. The government had not acknowledged the internment, and public recognition of a century-long Japanese Canadian presence remained politically sensitive. The application was amended to construct a “New Community Hall”.

The appeal was approved, and in 1978 Broader Horizons presented Mrs Kamegaya and the Kyowakai Society with an Award of Achievement. A national redress settlement would not come until ten years later.

Centennial Park in New Denver is named after this centennial milestone.

Kyowakai Hall

In 1943, Japanese Canadian carpenters built this hall. The British Columbia Security Commission ordered it demolished, deeming a large building for internees unacceptable.

Among those interned in New Denver was a Buddhist “temple carpenter”, trained from an early age in the ceremonial construction of a butsudan, a Buddhist shrine. He built a butsudan into the hall, helping ensure the building would remain standing as a place of worship.

As the largest building serving thousands of Japanese Canadian internees in the area, the hall hosted Buddhist, Christian, and secular ceremonies, weddings, and funerals, as well as education, community gatherings, and banquets.

The hall served as the meeting place for the Kyowakai (“working together peacefully”) Society until 1977, when the Centennial Hall was constructed.

The butsudan is still owned by the Jodo Shinshu Buddhist Association. Its Obon ceremony has been held here annually since 1943.

Heiwa Tien (Peace Garden)

The Heiwa Tien Peace Garden was created in 1993 by world-renowned master gardener Roy Tomomichi Sumi, who was previously interned in New Denver. This was Master Sumi’s final garden, designed in the kare-sansui (“dry landscape”) style, using raked gravel to evoke water currents, impermanence, and the ever-changing nature of life.

Heiwa Tien follows Japanese Canadians through a journey of yesterday, today, and tomorrow. From the British Columbia headwaters (yesterday), north of the Kyowakai Hall, the symbolic water cannot flow directly to the sea. Instead, it is diverted (today), forced to change course, winding under two footbridges before reaching the “lagoon” (tomorrow).

Master Sumi visited the Nikkei Internment Memorial Centre three times, sharing his process with villagers as they gathered. He began with barren, hard-packed ground. Boulders were selected in Sandon and placed by hand, precisely as he directed. He brought coastal plants for yesterday, invited seniors to donate plants that had grown in New Denver when they first were interned for today, and left the plantings around tomorrow, the lagoon, to evolve over time.

Master Roy Sumi died in 1997. He made his last visit after the Nikkei Internment Memorial Centre opened.
